Problem statement:

*Welcome to Antallagma - a digital exchange for trading goods. Antallagma started its operations 5 years back and has supported more than a million transactions till date. The Antallagma platform enables working of a traditional exchange on an online portal.

On one hand, buyers make a bid at the value they are willing to buy (bid value) and the quantity they are willing to buy. Sellers on the other hand, ask for an ask price and the quantity they are willing to sell. The portal matches the buyers and sellers in realtime to create trades. All trades are settled at the end of the day at the median price of all agreed trades.

You are one of the traders on the exchange and can supply all the material being traded on the exchange. In order to improve your logistics, you want to predict the median trade prices and volumes for all the trades happening (at item level) on the exchange. You can then plan to then use these predictions to create an optimized inventory strategy.

You are expected to create trade forecasts for all items being traded on Antallagma along with the trade prices for a period of 6 months. *

So, Being a supplier, we need to predict two things, the median trade prices and volumes of each item traded using machine learning. The behaviour of each item of buyers/ sellers can make significant predictors if captured properly. This can be translated into bidding/trading systems. Lets now have a look at my solution.

8. What worked and what didnt.

  1. Mean and standard deviation of the the outcome for each ItemID were strong predictors. This also makes sense as there one might expect only small fluctualations over the mean price which is captured preety well by the mean and standard deviations at ItemID level.

  2. Simple mean worked better than non-linear approach of Summation of outcomes for each Item id / (1 + log(count of trades by for that Item Id)). This sugegsts that less penalisation of the count of trades for each outcome at ItemID level is not nessasarily a good strategy, suggesting the no. of trades for each ItemId isnt a significant predictor.

  3. Day of week and is weekend flag when removed, improved the model performance suggesting day of week isnt nessasirily a strong predictor which is strange in many ways. This probably could mean this is data for bidding market and masked features like say category 1 might the no. of contenders interested in the bidding.

  4. Taking the log(1+x) transformation of the outcomes served well as it helped to remove the skeweness to a large extent.

  5. Mean of the outcome served as a better predictor than the median, which is slighly strange as we were predicting median trade prices as the outcome. This might be because the evaluation metric is RMSE and median might be directing us away from the mean.
